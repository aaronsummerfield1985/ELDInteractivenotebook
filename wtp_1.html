Lesson One: Background

Why do we need this unit?

Writing to a prompt can feel daunting, especially in high-stakes settings like tests, scholarships, or job applications. Sometimes you have plenty of time, but other times you have on-demand tasks with limited time.

The task is clear: address the prompt. Don't get distracted by adding details that don't directly answer the points called out.

Whether you are backing up an opinion, describing a game, or explaining science, the paragraph essay structure works every time.

Lesson One: The Formula

Academic Writing Formula

1. Introduce the topic
2. Develop the topic
3. Provide a conclusion / wrap up your response

This formula is your game plan for success!

Step 2. Deconstruct the prompt

Find out what you are being asked to do.

Highlight imperative verbs (commands).

Identify main instructions and read each sentence carefully.

Lesson One: Process

Building confidence step by step (3 & 4)

Step 3. Create an outline

Plan your formula application: Introduce, Develop, Conclude.

Cross-check with the prompt and rubric to ensure every detail is included.

Step 4. Write your essay

Follow your outline using full sentences.

  • Check your tone and transitions.
  • Verify spelling and formatting.
  • Stay focused on the question!

Section One, Lesson Three

Mentor Text: Prompt One (Part 1)

It was a calm, crisp evening in April. I was taking an evening hike, like I usually do whenever the weather is nice. Everyone in my community knows that hiking is my passion and I always try to drag a friend along with me. But on this day, the weather was chillier than normal and I had to go by myself. I had forgotten my beanie and it was getting dark, so I decided to cut it short. As I was heading down the hill, I heard a screeching sound echoing throughout the canyon. My heart stopped for a moment as I wondered what it could be.

Just as the sun was about to set, I caught a glimpse of the most beautiful bird-like creature I've ever seen. Their large body was a shimmery white that looked like the snow on the mountain behind them. They had long, glistening feathers running down their back. They had a tail that was as long as a car and two strong hind legs with long claws. The animal's tail and legs were covered with silver and gold scales. Two striped, pointed ears popped up from the top of their head. Their face looked like a snow owl and as they turned their head, I saw a small beak poking out from their profile. There was an orange glow around their body as they reflected the sun setting behind them. They looked so majestic.

Lesson Three: Mentor Text

Mentor Text: Prompt One (Part 2)

I took out my phone to take a picture. I was worried the creature would be able to hear me moving, but they continued to munch on the leaves around them. Suddenly, they let out the loud, hair-raising screech I had heard earlier. The animal finally heard me sneaking around the tree, trying to get a better glimpse. Our eyes locked! To my surprise, the creature slowly walked toward me with a smile-like expression on their face. I felt like they were greeting me and letting me know that I was safe. Once they were close enough, I reached out to touch the feathers that looked as soft as clouds. Right before I could touch them, they turned around and disappeared into the woods.

I ran home, so excited that I had seen such a magical being. I had been hiking around their home almost every day for the last two years. I believe they knew that I really loved their home and that I wouldn't do anything to harm them. I can only imagine what else might be living right under my nose. Maybe one day I'll find out.

Section One, Lesson Five

Understanding Rubric Criteria

Organization/Purpose

Skillfully includes all prompt requirements: Beginning (Introduce characters/setting), Middle (Rising action), End (Resolution).

Development/Elaboration

Events and experiences are clearly developed and seamlessly advance the plot.

Language

Skillfully uses descriptive and figurative language and time phrases throughout the story.

Conventions

Uses wide variety of complex, compound, and simple sentences. Effective command of punctuation, capitalization, and spelling.

Lesson Five: Language

Sentence Types

Simple

One independent clause (Subject + Predicate). Makes sense on its own.

Compound

Two independent clauses joined by comma/conjunction or semicolon.

Complex

One independent clause + at least one dependent clause.

Reflect: Which sentence types did you use in your draft?
